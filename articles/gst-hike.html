What the GST Hike Really Costs a University Student

Rising food prices, higher utility bills, and a stagnant bursary system — we ran the numbers on what Singapore's tax changes mean for your monthly budget.

RM
Ravi Menon
18 April 2026 · 6 min read
Key figure
A full-time NUS student spending $1,200/month now faces an estimated $340 increase in annual GST-attributable costs. Bursary top-ups cover less than 40% of that.

When Singapore raised the GST to 9% in January 2024, the Ministry of Finance assured residents that support packages would cushion the blow. For working adults with CPF balances and rebates to tap, the math more or less holds. For university students, it doesn't.

Students typically don't file income tax. That means they don't receive GST Vouchers — the primary offset mechanism for lower-income households. Unless they live with their parents (and many don't), the hike hits without a buffer.

Where the money actually goes

We broke down a typical student monthly budget of $1,200 across the main spending categories and calculated the GST impact on each. The biggest single contributor isn't tuition or rent — it's food.

Hawker centre meals and food court purchases are technically GST-exempt when bought from non-GST-registered stalls. But most campus food outlets, supermarkets, and convenience stores are registered, which means the hike passes through directly. We estimate food centre spending alone accounts for roughly $180 of the annual increase for a student eating on campus five days a week.

"Food centres account for roughly $180 of the increase — a category that receives no GST offset for students who don't file income tax."

Utilities are the second largest contributor. Students in hall or rental rooms often pay utilities directly or through a landlord who passes on costs. The 2% increase on a $120 monthly utility bill adds $28.80 per year — modest in isolation, but compounding alongside everything else.

What the bursary system covers

NUS, NTU, and SMU all expanded their bursary schemes in 2024 in response to the GST increase. The top-up amounts range from $200 to $500 per year depending on household income band. For a student in the $3,500–$4,499 per capita household income band — the middle tier — the top-up is $200.

That covers roughly 59% of our estimated $340 increase. The gap is not catastrophic. But it is real, and it compounds year-on-year for students who remain in the same income band across three or four years of study.

What you can do about it

The most actionable steps are administrative. First, check your bursary eligibility every academic year — income bands shift as family circumstances change. Second, register for the Community Development Council vouchers, which are available to all Singapore residents regardless of income tax filing status. Third, for students who do hold part-time work, filing a tax return — even at zero income — creates an income tax record that unlocks GST Voucher eligibility going forward.

None of this fully offsets the increase. But understanding the structure of where the cost lands is the first step to managing it.
